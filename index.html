Satyajit Ray

1921 - 1992
                    Ray was an Indian director, screenwriter, documentary filmmaker, author, polymath, polyglot, essayist, lyricist, music composer, magazine
                    editor, illustrator, calligrapher, and composer. He is widely considered as one of the greatest film-makers of all time.
                    Satyajit Ray was born in Calcutta on May 2, 1921. His father, Late Sukumar Ray was an eminent poet and writer in the history of Bengali
                    literature. In 1940, after receiving his degree in science and economics from Calcutta University, he attended Tagore's Viswa-Bharati
                    University. Starting his career as a commercial artist, Ray was drawn into independent film-making after meeting French filmmaker
                    Jean Renoir and viewing Vittorio De Sica's Italian neorealist film Bicycle Thieves (1948) during a visit to London.
                    Ray directed 36 films, including feature films, documentaries, and shorts. He is well known for his contributions to Bengali literature.
                    He created two of the most famous characters in Feluda the sleuth and Professor Shanku the scientist. He wrote several short novels and
                    stories in addition to those based on these two characters. His fiction was targeted mainly at younger readers, though it became popular
                    among children and adults alike.
                    Ray designed four typefaces for roman script named Ray Roman, Ray Bizarre, Daphnis, and Holiday script, apart from numerous Bengali ones
                    for the Sandesh magazine.[113][114] Ray Roman and Ray Bizarre won an international competition in 1971. In certain circles of Calcutta,
                    Ray continued to be known as an eminent graphic designer, well into his film career. He illustrated all his books and designed covers for
                    them, as well as creating all publicity material for his films.
                    He died on 23rd April, 1992 in Kolkata, at the age of 70 due to heart and lung ailments.





THE APU TRILOGY

Ray made his directoral debut in 1955 with an Indian Bengali-language drama film, named Pather Panchali, which was also written by him. It is an adaptation of
Bibhutibhushan Bandyopadhyay's 1929 Bengali novel of the same name. Ray sold all his possessions to make his first film. Featuring Subir Banerjee, Kanu Banerjee,
Karuna Banerjee, Uma Dasgupta, Pinaki Sengupta, Chunibala Devi and being the first film in The Apu Trilogy, Pather Panchali depicts the childhood travails of the protagonist Apu
and his elder sister Durga amidst the harsh village life of their poor family.
Pather Panchali won eleven international prizes, including the inaugural Best Human Document award at the 1956 Cannes Film Festival. This film, along with Aparajito (1956) and
Apur Sansar (The World of Apu) (1959), form The Apu Trilogy. Ray did the scripting, casting, scoring, and editing, and designed his own credit titles and publicity material.
He won the Lifetime Academy Award from the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences at the Oscars in 1992. The award, which he could not receive in person because he was ailing,
was given to him "in recognition of his rare mastery of the art of motion pictures and for his profound humanitarian outlook, which has had an indelible influence on filmmakers
and audiences throughout the world."




OTHER NOTABLE WORKS OF RAY

FELUDA
It is an Indian-Bengali detective media franchise created by Ray. He wrote 35 published and 4 unpublished stories and directed two Feluda movies — Sonar Kella (1974) and Joi Baba Felunath (1978). After his death, his only son, Sandip Ray, made a new Feluda film series (continuation of the original series) on Feluda's adventures.
 CHARULATA
Charulata is a 1964 Indian drama film written and directed by Satyajit Ray. Based upon the novel Nastanirh by Rabindranath Tagore, it stars Soumitra Chatterjee, Madhabi Mukherjee and Sailen Mukherjee. The film is considered one of the finest works of Ray.
DEVI
Devi (1960), a Bengali-language Hindu drama film, starring debutante Sharmila Tagore and Soumitra Chatterjee, is based on a short story by Provatkumar Mukhopadhyay. The film is based on the worship of women and young girls as incarnations of the goddesses Durga or Kali, which is more prevalent in Nepal as the Kumari tradition.		 GOOPY–BAGHA
Goopy–Bagha is a series of Indian Bengali fantasy adventure comedy films based on a story by Satyajit Ray's grandfather Upendrakishore Ray Chowdhury. Ray directed the first two films of the series, Gupi Gyne Bagha Byne and Heerak Rajar Deshe, while the third Gupi Bagha Phire Elo was directed by his son. The trilogy starred Tapen Chatterjee and Rabi Ghosh as Goopy and Bagha respectively.
ARANYER DIN RATRI
It is an Indian Bengali adventure drama film released in 1970, written and directed by Satyajit Ray. It is based upon the Bengali novel of the same name by Sunil Gangopadhyay. It had an eminent cast including Soumitra Chatterjee, Subhendu Chatterjee, Rabi Ghosh, Aparna Sen, Sharmila Tagore and many others. The film was nominated for the Golden Bear for Best Film at the 20th Berlin International Film Festival.		 NAYAK
It is a 1966 drama film which was composed, written, and directed by Satyajit Ray and marked his second entirely original screenplay. The story revolves around a matinee idol on a 24-hour train journey from Kolkata to Delhi who reveals his mistakes, insecurities and regrets to a young journalist, who realises that behind all his arrogant facade lies a deeply troubled man. The film starring Uttam Kumar and Sharmila Tagore.

AWARDS, HONOURS AND RECOGNITIONS

  • Ray received many awards, including 36 National Film Awards by the Government of India, and awards at International Film Festivals.
    • 
            
  • At the 11th Moscow International Film Festival in 1979, he was awarded with the Honorable Prize for the contribution to cinema.
    • 
            
  • At the Venice Film Festival, he was awarded the Golden Lion Honorary Award in 1982.
    • 
            
  • That same year, he received an honorary "Hommage à Satyajit Ray" award at the 1982 Cannes Film Festival.
    • 
            
  • Ray is the second film personality after Charlie Chaplin to have been awarded an honorary doctorate by Oxford University.
    • 
            
  • He was awarded the Dadasaheb Phalke Award in 1985, and the Legion of Honor by the President of France in 1987.
    • 
            
  • The Government of India awarded him the Padma Bhushan in 1965 and the highest civilian honour, Bharat Ratna, shortly before his death.
    • 
            
  • In 1992, he was awarded the Akira Kurosawa Award for Lifetime Achievement in directing at the San Francisco International Film Festival.
    • 
            
  • In 1992, the Sight & Sound Critics' Top Ten Poll ranked Ray at No. 7 in its list of "Top 10 Directors" of all time, making him the highest-ranking
Asian filmmaker in the poll.
    • 
            
  • In 2022, the Sydney Film Festival showcased 10 films by Ray as homage and the BFI Southbank screened a complete retrospective in July.
